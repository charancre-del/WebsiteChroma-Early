Occupational Therapy

Skills for the
Job of Living.

For a child, their "occupation" is playing, learning, and growing. Our Occupational Therapists (OTs) build the foundational skills needed for independence, sensory regulation, and confidence.

How It Works Book Consultation

Independence

Focus Areas

We address the underlying challenges that impact a child's ability to participate fully in daily life.

Fine Motor Skills

Improving grasp, dexterity, and coordination for tasks like writing, cutting, and using utensils.

Sensory Processing

Helping children organize sensory input (sound, touch, movement) to stay calm and regulated.

Self-Care (ADLs)

Building independence in daily routines: dressing, feeding, brushing teeth, and toileting.

Visual Motor

Coordinating what the eyes see with what the hands do—crucial for reading and throwing a ball.

The Process

Building a Foundation for Growth

1

Evaluation

We use standardized assessments (like the Peabody or Beery VMI) and clinical observation to understand your child's motor and sensory profile.

2

Sensory Diet & Plan

We create a personalized plan that may include a "sensory diet"—activities to do at home or school to keep your child regulated.

3

Play-Based Intervention

Sessions are fun! We use swings, obstacle courses, putty, and crafts to work on hard skills without it feeling like "work."

4

Home Carryover

We empower you with strategies to help with picky eating, bedtime struggles, and homework frustration at home.

Service Settings

Sensory Gyms Swings, climbing walls & crash pads

Common Questions About OT

My child is clumsy. Can OT help?

Yes. What looks like clumsiness is often a challenge with "proprioception" (body awareness) or motor planning. We work on coordination through obstacle courses and balance activities.

Do you help with picky eating?

Absolutely. Feeding therapy is a specialized part of OT. We use the SOS (Sequential Oral Sensory) approach to help children explore new textures and foods without pressure.

Is this just playing?

It looks like play, but it is "purposeful play." Every activity is clinically designed to challenge a specific muscle group, sensory system, or cognitive skill while keeping your child motivated.
